Examples of site types across Al Ula:


Inscriptions and Rock Art

The Al Ula region is rich in inscriptions and rock art from many different periods. The rock art most often depicts animals, like ibex and camels, but also humans, and for example hunting or fighting scenes. While sometimes difficult to date exactly, the rock art and inscriptions appear to represent periods from prehistory through Nabataean times right up to the present.

Hejaz Railway

The Damascus-Medina Hejaz Railway was constructed between 1900 and 1908 and the section to Al Ula officially opened on 1 September 1907. A major railway station complex was built at Mada’in Saleh, and large facilities were provided at Al Ula. Railway stations, bridges, and the track-bed still provide tangible evidence. The Hejaz Railway is a candidate for the UNESCO World Heritage list.

Gates

A ‘gate’ is an arrangement of walls and cairns, forming the shape of a field gate when seen from above, first identified through remote sensing. Very little is known about them, but they are thought to be related to funerary and/or memorial practices. They are believed to date back to prehistory; excavations as part of IDIHA will hopefully shed more light on this.

A selection of sites from Al Ula:


Al Ula Old Town

This town consisting of mud brick houses is probably at least a 1000 years old and was still in use in the 1970s. Buildings are typically two floors, constructed from sandstone, mudbrick, and palm trees, and usually one of the upper rooms hangs over the street, thereby shading it. Most of the roofs are now collapsed, but many walls are still standing.

Dadan (al-Khuraybah)

The famous Iron Age, Nabataean and Early Islamic site of Dadan (al-Khuraybah). The site contains a settlement, including temples and wells, and tombs

Funerary Complex

Survey site AU01803 is a funerary complex containing a ringed tower tomb and a triangular structure oriented towards it.

Rock Art

Survey site AU01693 is one of the many examples of rock art in the region. It is a large rock art panel with over eighteen ibexes and other animals.

Rock Art

Survey site AU01905 is a very large panel with multiple rock art images, including cattle, and inscriptions in several North Arabian scripts.

Ringed Tower Tomb

Survey site AU01742 is a ringed tower tomb with clear, linear arranged tail features. There have been test excavations of the tomb.

Gate

Site AU01696 is an example of a so-called 'gate'. In this case the remains on the surface and imagery were not very impressive, but when excavated the site turned out to be very interesting.

Gate

Site AU01702 is another example of a 'gate' site. The parallel and perpendicular walls from which the site type derives its name are clearly visible on satellite and aerial imagery.

Funerary Complex

Survey site AU01770 is an example of a funerary, or burial, complex with a ringed tower tomb, a triangular structure, and multiple 'tail' features. Such funerary complexes are common in the Al Ula region.

Funerary Complex

Survey site AU01597 is an example of a funerary complex. It contains a ringed cairn, probably a tomb, and related features such as graves. Its location right next to the road makes it easily accessibe but also more vulnerable to disturbances.

Al Ula Station

This large Hejaz Railway station, located at 980 km from Damascus, was officially opened on September 1st 1907. It comprises several two-story buildings, in a style typical of large stations further north along the railway. During the Arab Revolt (1916-1918) it was a British bombing target, causing significant damage.

Al-Thamid

The site of al-Thamid, survey site AU03009, represents extensive Ottoman fortifications, including a long, over 500m long defensive wall and gun emplacements.
